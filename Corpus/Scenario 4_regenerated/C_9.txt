Southwest of Windstoke, Willowfen stretches across marshy wetlands where willows dip trailing branches into brackish water. Wooden boardwalks zigzag over mudflats, enabling safe passage past clusters of reeds that rustle in unison with each breeze. Egrets and herons stalk the shallows at dawn, silhouettes outlined against pale skies. Fishermen cast lines from shallow punts, drifting quietly through twisting channels that reflect drifting clouds. Late morning reveals a lively floating market where vendors peddle eels, pickled vegetables, and woven reed baskets. A hush blankets the fen by midday, broken only by frogs croaking beneath thick algae. Occasionally, small huts perch on stilts, their inhabitants skilled at net-mending or brewing potent herbal tonics from rare marsh blooms. Sunset paints the waters gold, coaxing fireflies from the reeds in twinkling arcs. Locals recall tales of ghostly lights drifting among the cattails, attributed to restless spirits or natural gas pockets—no one quite agrees. Families gather near central causeways for open-air feasts, feasting on spiced stews with local crayfish. Children chase one another with homemade lanterns, giggling at the reflection dancing below. Willowfen’s nights can be eerily serene: the quiet so profound that even a single splash echoes wide. A watchtower stands on a firm patch of ground, though its sentinel is rarely needed outside spring flood season.
