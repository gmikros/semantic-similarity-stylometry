Beyond Willowfen’s marshes, the Grand Moorland unfurls in windswept expanses of heather and peat bogs marked by solitary pines. Dawn’s light creeps across this terrain, revealing deer grazing near shimmering pockets of water. Occasionally, hidden peat-cutter huts appear, stacked with sod bricks drying under pale skies. Ponies roam free, their manes rippling in chilly gusts that rattle low shrubs. By midmorning, hardy dwellers gather in a small cluster of dwellings on the moor’s edge to swap stories over steaming mugs of nettle tea. Weather dominates conversation: a shift in wind can herald sudden storms rolling across open ground. Sparse trees bend into dramatic shapes, testaments to ceaseless gales. Visitors may stumble upon ancient standing stones, etched with runes whose meaning vanished centuries ago. At twilight, the horizon becomes a canvas of fiery streaks, silhouettes of silent watchers perched on rock outcroppings. Legends tell of will-o’-the-wisps guiding wanderers astray, yet locals follow carefully placed cairns to navigate. Despite the harshness, small wonders abound: tiny flowers clinging to spongy peat or unexpected waterfalls forming in rocky gullies. Nighttime hush descends like a soft cloak, unveiling constellations rarely glimpsed in brighter lands. Tales pass among residents of ghostly processions seen against moonlit hills, though skeptics claim illusions by swirling fog. The Moorland’s raw beauty fosters a sense of reverence, forging deep bonds among those who endure its extremes.
